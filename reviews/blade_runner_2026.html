Blade Runner

March 7, 2026 - 9:13 AM - Standard

New consciousness.

More human than human.

Will we ever inhabit a world where human-like computers, known as replicants, live amongst us? In most cases, they would be indistinguishable in look and feel. They would speak to us, cry with us, and listen to our stories. If harmed, they would bleed. We’d fear them too: Among replicants’ human traits is that they can act on their vengeance. Replicants are born with the complexity of our species.

In the world as it was in 1982, Blade Runner presented a distant future. Back then, technology was not so ingrained in our culture. Replicants were not an impending discovery.

Today, personal computers rest in our pockets, the internet follows us everywhere, and the perils of artificial intelligence are tabletop conversation in most corners of the Earth. The technology exists now, so who will put it all together?

It took forty-four years, but a modern audience is bringing a new context to this film. There is an edge to the dystopia of Los Angeles in 2019, where many humans have gone off-world and left behind a polluted, decrepit Earth. In LA, the sky is so dark, not even the sun breaks through. The film’s gloominess speaks to the cultural moment. That’s when the replicants start to feel more real than fiction.

Harrison Ford plays Rick Deckard, a police officer with one job: Retire replicants. He is known as a Blade Runner. This isn’t the noble hero that Ford became famous for portraying. Han Solo was motivated by money and eventually a cause. Indiana Jones was driven by the thrill of discovery and the preservation of history.

And Deckard? He drinks during all times of the day, wracked with existential questions. A replicant is made from silicon and other metals as computers are. Yet, they develop memories, feelings, and a purpose. Does their construction matter? Eventually, society must reckon with the ultimate question: Are replicants human?

Blade Runner poses this ultimate question as a philosophical undercurrent running through the entire film. It is built into Deckard’s character arc; the more replicants Deckard retires, the more he considers the action itself.

Deckard executes violent replicants, but even violent criminals stand trial. Humans enslave replicants off-world for free labor, but human slavery is outlawed. If replicants are human, the systems would unravel.

If a question serves as Blade Runner’s core, then the film’s gritty atmosphere is what compels us to consider it. As a piece of science fiction, it has the cliché concept of flying cars, but undoes the trope by surrounding them with shadow. Pure, saturated colors are only seen in neon, but the main palette is desaturated or even blown out by bright, white spotlights. It reinforces the artificiality of Earth — all the lush greenery, trees, and animals, that we take for granted, have been replaced by human creations.

Multicultural advertisements fill the skyline billboards: Pan Am and Coca-Cola share the spotlight with Japanese geishas swallowing pills. On the ground, Deckard sees people from all walks of life. Vangelis’s score, which includes synthesizer, jazz, vocals in an Arabic-like language, and Indian instruments, is truly a global soundtrack.

In all this, there is another prescient idea: Society will converge towards a monoculture, which combines elements from all. Director Ridley Scott was not wrong: Kids all over the world are singing songs from a movie called K-Pop Demon Hunters. We’re trending that way.

Initially, I felt that the world of Blade Runner was bereft of emotion. I realize now that I was looking in the wrong place. Its emotion lies in the question: Are replicants human? That is the true access point into what this film is about.

When you look at each of Deckard’s action, it paints the question with a new layer. Deckard falls in love with Rachael (Sean Young), an advanced replicant. Can a replicant love him back?

Deckard retires multiple replicants who try to escape their fate. Do his actions carry the same moral weight as terminating humans? Is there a soul underneath the machinery?

Rachael asks Deckard a simple question: Have you ever retired a human by mistake? Clearly, what makes something human is not flesh, intelligence, or culture. The replicants have all of it. The separation rests in one elusive component: Can they feel others’ emotions?

That is the purpose of the Voight-Kampff test in Blade Runner, which determines whether a test subject can feel empathy. Replicants are presented with open-ended situations, designed to trigger emotional responses from humans. Since replicants are not given any memories, they tend to become confused, irritated, and aggressive.

But some replicants can almost pass the last remaining test. Those that have implanted memories (like Rachael) or carry traumatic experiences (like the crew Deckard is assigned to retire) are different.

The picture investigates this question until it delivers an answer at the close.

Roy (Rutger Hauer), a replicant who perpetrated a genocide against humans off-world, has Rick Deckard hanging from a rooftop. Roy has witnessed the death of his entire replicant crew, including the replicant he loved, by Deckard’s hand.

With his only remaining obstacle about to plummet to his death, Roy reaches down, lifts Deckard by his arm, and drops him onto the roof. Deckard backs away, frightened of the beating to come. But Roy collapses to his knees, breathing heavily — and constructs a poem in real-time.

  • ROY
  • I've seen things you people wouldn't believe. Attack ships on fire off the shoulder of Orion. I watched C-beams glitter in the dark near the Tannhäuser Gate. All those moments will be lost in time, like tears in rain. Time to die.

His heads falls. He is no more. The expiration date that his creator set has arrived.

For a short-lived machine, Roy’s poem is fitting. It includes vibrant details, while recognizing that his life approaches its limit. He leaves us, questioning: Why would a replicant like Roy not exact vengeance?

At the close, Roy wanted someone to witness his passing so he might be remembered. Deckard, the villain of his life, is better than no one at all. It is the most human of desires reflected in a machine. Roy confirms that replicants do feel emotion. But that’s not all.

In those moments when Deckard is hanging from the roof, Roy shakes with rage, wearing a murderous look. Roy is to Deckard what a human with a magnifying glass is to an ant on a sunny day.

Then, Ridley Scott offers the first close-up of Roy, so we witness his realization. A spotlight highlights his face — and his pupils dilate. A recognition emerges of what Deckard is experiencing: He fears for his life.

  • ROY
  • Quite an experience to live in fear, isn't it. That’s what it is to be a slave.

Ridley Scott uses a series of rapid cuts between the two characters. A close-up on Roy’s eyes, a shot of Deckard hanging, another close-up of Roy, Deckard’s hands giving out, then — the grab! Roy calculated the last possible moment to save Deckard. He was merciful, but he wanted the lesson to stick.

When Roy recognizes his own emotions in Deckard, he chooses to save Deckard from that fate. Roy proves that replicants can feel empathy.

The end of Blade Runner disintegrates the illusion that replicants are not human. Another question must follow: Can you imagine a world where humans and replicants are equals?

One day, we will have replicants that make perfectly human decisions, with flesh, intelligence, culture, and emotion. But humans will discriminate: Are you made from carbon or silicon? This fact about material composition will become another fault-line like sex, race, or religion.

We must choose: Can a replicant lawfully be employed and earn money? Can a replicant own a home or get married? Can a replicant be a citizen? Replicants will learn everything they know from us. As we do, they will yearn to be free, to not be hunted for being different.

Blade Runner has the wisdom of foresight. The author, Phillip K. Dick, who penned the source material knew we would judge human-like computers for being made of inorganic material like silicon, not organics like us. He tried to warn us in 1968. Ridley Scott picked up the baton in 1982.

They both arrived at the same conclusion: That day is coming.
