Society of the Snow

January 17, 2024 - 11:30 PM - Standard

Make it count.

You have permission to use my body.

Spoilers Ahead

Society of the Snow is a film that pushes the audience to the limit of acceptable experience. In a dramatic interpretation of a real-life event, our cast of characters are Uruguayan survivors of a plane crash, living in the remaining wreckage somewhere in the Andes Mountains. With artistic direction, the emotions that the characters feel find their way to the audience: distress, nail-biting tension, and absolute dread.

With the wonderful eye of director J.A. Bayona, Society of the Snow achieves the gold standard of the survival film: to place the audience beside characters facing their own mortality. The specificity of this true story has produced images that cannot be struck from my mind: a leg crushed by the force of a plane crash; survivors ascending a mountain slope like zombies; teeth loosened through malnutrition; frozen bodies emerging out of the snow during the sunrise; hands warmed through self-urination. In a way that no news article or book can do, this group’s unfathomable reality is recreated with tremendous detail and emotional heft.

The film uses practical locations to consume the characters in a winter abyss. Even if the characters scale a massive peak, where do we go from there? The breadth of the Andes Mountain Range is its own obstacle to survival. There must be CGI hidden in this film; it is an achievement that it never jerked us out of our misery.

After enduring setback after setback, finally, there is a rescue, powered by overwhelming feelings of happiness, cheer, and sweeping orchestral music. When it happens in Society of the Snow, it is deserved. The plight of these characters has been our emotional plight; now, their euphoria is our delight. It does not last.

It is a common trope in survival movies that we know the protagonist will find a way to survive. They are special to us. We believe in them – until they are very painfully taken away from us. In Society of the Snow, we lose Numa (Enzo Vogrincic). As an actor, Vogrincic is magnetic in his scenes; we immediately stick to his performance and root for his character to find a way, even if it ultimately means trading dignity for sustenance. It is only when Numa dies that we emotionally reckon with the magnitude of human loss among these survivors.

Sixteen people survived through over sixty days of freezing nights and terrible choices. Twenty-nine did not. After experiencing this story, I had forgotten for a time what was happening in my own reality. Mundane activities like drinking water, turning on a heater, or sleeping in a warm bed stopped feeling so ordinary.

Narration in films almost always comes from living characters, not the dead. Throughout the film and especially after the survivors make it back to civilization, Society of the Snow speaks through one of the lost souls of Uruguayan Air Force Flight 571: Numa. The point of his concluding narration is you are alive. That is a precious opportunity.

The young members of this flight were heading out for a fun weekend – and got something they never imagined. Make it count.
