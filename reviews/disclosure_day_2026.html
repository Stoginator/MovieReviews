Tanish's Movie Reviews

Disclosure Day

June 14, 2026 - 4:32 PM - IMAX Laser

The magic of a filmmaker.

Listen.

A breathtaking assortment of stories awaits in the filmmaking canon of director Steven Spielberg. He is known for fictional stories of extraordinary events: a shark attacking citizens of a small island in 1975's Jaws or a suburban boy meeting an alien in 1982's E.T. The Extra Terrestrial. His stories of late are equally captivating. Fiction or non-fiction, they offer encoded messages about our world.

Disclosure Day operates at this intersection as a fictional story with compelling characters, who are in situations with very real-world consequences. It entertains us with an palpable edge, a constant sense that all of this could be real, happening in plain sight, outside of public knowledge. I wonder: How close have we come to a disclosure day?

To say more would ruin the fun and the big questions that await audiences who enter into dimly-lit auditoriums, ready for a grand adventure. I had the good fortune of viewing this film with my parents, who introduced me to Spielberg's works as a boy. During the film, I looked over at my father, whose eyes were completely transfixed by this story. The magic of Spielberg never fails.

Spoilers Ahead

The continuum of Spielberg's work has led us to Disclosure Day, a film that uses his earliest instincts to tell stories about humans and extraterrestrials and combines it with his post-9/11 fascination with stories that test the boundaries of American values. He uses films to speak directly to us.

The protagonists are private citizens, continuing the main theme present throughout Spielberg's canon: Ordinary people thrown into extraordinary circumstances. They have come into the knowledge of "a core truth about our world," that we are not alone, that we share the universe. The revelation compels them to disclose this truth to the world.

Dan Kellner (Josh O'Connor) has the evidence. He parallels Edward Snowden as a sophisticated programmer that leaks public documents in the name of the public good. His girlfriend, June, is a lapsed Christian, who almost became a nun. Afraid of embarrassing herself in today's society, she hides this background but uses it to asks him probing questions:

  1. Should the truth be revealed at all?
  2. Why are you the one to reveal this?
  3. Can humanity handle two Supreme Beings, one being God and the other being aliens?

Disclosure Day leans into the consequential questions of extraterrestrial disclosure, with many characters operating at opposite ends of the debate. Their battling conversations, where we simultaneously learn about their past and their decision-making process, are riveting.

In the background of possible disclosure, the world is reacting to impending, global conflict. New outlets cover military preparations between the United States and Russia, referring to events as "World War III." Gas stations are being mobbed, with people cleaning out shelves and stocking up on fuel. During this new crisis, the hoarding mentality of COVID-19 reemerges.

Yet, Disclosure Day is not completely dreary. Spielberg highlights the good in America: He consciously chose to set the film entirely in Kansas City, affirming the value of places we rarely see in our popular entertainment. A protagonist, Margaret (Emily Blunt), is a local weather-woman on KCPE, spotlighting local news at a time when the information firehose is dominated by national or global stories.

Disclosure Day's opening scene is a major surprise: A first-person view of being kicked by a pro wrestler during a match. In this environment, a private corporation, Wardex, operates in plain sight, trying to obtain the most sensitive, classified data ever handled. The setting is purposeful: Without our assent, a covert force with unaligned goals has become pervasive in our society.

In 1960, President Eisenhower issued a warning to Americans to limit the power gained "the military-industrial complex." Why? Such organizations can become unaccountable, ghost networks. They operate from the inside, working in the shadows, using their full knowledge of our laws in order to evade them.

In Disclosure Day, that is precisely what we see. The Wardex Corporation is hidden from public scrutiny; their work paid for by taxpayers, yet completely classified. They have 79 years of evidence about extraterrestrials locked away, even from the highest leaders of American government. Wardex exemplifies what happens when too much power is transferred from elected representatives to a private corporation.

Wardex uses alien technology, not for the betterment of mankind, but to surveil American citizens in their homes, during their most private moments. There is no court-ordered search warrant, only Wardex's complete disregard for 4th Amendment protections. The contempt for liberties may be beyond even President Eisenhower's imagination. In Spielberg's earlier films like E.T., governments used to overreach; now, his anxieties arise from private entities.

When the aliens are finally revealed onscreen, I found myself puzzled. They seemed stereotypical, matching the image of aliens that pop up on Google Images. Then, I realized that is exactly the point. Disclosure Day suggests that the numerous conspiracies and unconfirmed stories come from a truthful place, but have been covered up. Naturally, it follows the image of aliens in the popular imagination are also sincere.

Spielberg's primary contention about aliens is that they would do no harm. Throughout his career, he has never made a version of 1979's Alien or 1996's Independence Day where extraterrestrials attack humans. In Spielberg's films, they are non-violent, even when tortured. They come in peace.

Disclosure Day continues this assertion, ending on a message directly from an alien. It is freed from Wardex's captivity and offers the first public words to all of humanity. We only hear one word: "Listen." Then, the film cuts to black. From our point of view, its message is incomplete.

Truthfully, I was perplexed... the remainder was deliberately unrevealed. Why? I flipped through the events of the film for an answer – and I found one in Margaret, the local weather-woman from Kansas City.

Margaret is an ordinary woman, chosen by aliens to be part of the bridge between their kind and humanity. As a child, she was given the extraordinary powers of empathy, the greatest gift that aliens can bestow. This occurs during the celestial montage, a standout piece of filmmaking. As a sequence devoid of dialogue, it blends memory with the present, flowing like a dream. The context we need is inherent in the image, and our emotions are brought out by John William's fabulous score. We finally understand Margaret's powers.

Throughout the film, Margaret repeatedly astonishes those she meets, by speaking directly to their deepest desires. She voices their fears and validates them. Any conflict, even the disagreement about disclosure, simply falls away in the presence of someone like Margaret. She makes others feel deeply seen. Through Margaret, the aliens tell us that the solution to what ails us is the purest, most virtuous action: To listen.

Disclosure Day is Spielberg combining two halves of his career, using his most famous subject to communicate a message to American society. He gives us a call to action, within each citizen's power, that when practiced across the land, can transform a nation.
