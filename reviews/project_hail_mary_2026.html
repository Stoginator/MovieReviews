Tanish's Movie Reviews

Project Hail Mary

March 22, 2026 - 5:56 PM - IMAX Laser

Discovering courage in unexpected places.

I’m believing in you.

Spoilers Ahead

There’s a shot in Project Hail Mary that has stuck with me. We see a figure cloaked in shadow inside a tunnel, his face hidden from view. Beautiful orange light surrounds him on both ends. He walks towards us. As the music ascends, he escapes and the orange light illuminates his face, revealing his true emotions. Tears are swelling in his eyes. It is an important moment for Ryland Grace, the protagonist played by Ryan Gosling.

Grace has felt so alone, stranded somewhere deep in space, coping with a fate he didn't choose. He was sent on the most important mission there can be. Yet, he would be the first to admit he is the unlikeliest person tasked with saving the world.

Until this point, Grace has muddled through with luck, humor, and some amount of denial. He can't even remember how he got here, but he is. We understand his stoicism as pent-up emotions. And when he finally releases them, there's an implicit idea at work that is never announced: In hard times, it’s okay to cry.

Project Hail Mary is a story for our cultural moment. Originally written as a novel by Andy Weir, the writer of The Martian, Project Hail Mary repeatedly places its characters with their back against the wall. We never know how, or even if, our characters will escape their predicament. Hope feels hard to come by.

Seeing enormous potential in the novel, Ryan Gosling shepherded a dream team for the film adaptation. Drew Goddard, the same screenwriter who adapted the film version of The Martian, returned to pen Weir’s second big screen adaptation, keeping Weir's signature tone intact. Directors Phil Lord and Chris Miller share much of Weir's style in their previous works, so they knew how to seamlessly blend humor and tugs at our heart-strings. All of them shared a creative vision and kept it intact the whole way.

Project Hail Mary feels familar but fresh. The familiarity comes from the elements it borrows from popular works in science-fiction. There's the deep space travel and parallel Earth story found in Interstellar, the development of inter-species communication from Arrival, and the friendship from E.T. The Extra Terrestrial. The film recognizes our own ability to make these connections, by including self-aware nods to what came before from Superman to Close Encounters of the Third Kind.

The freshness comes from placing these elements inside Andy Weir's signature style – a plot driven by scientific accuracy, steady doses of humor, candid dialogue, and characters experiencing scientific discovery.

A new substance has been found in our solar system, which dims our sun. No one knows what it is. Particularly not Ryland Grace. As a middle school teacher with an unused PhD in molecular biology, why would anyone let him take a crack at it?

A blunt, secretive German bureaucrat (Sandra Hüller) enters his classroom in a rush. She thinks he should research it. That's the first surprise. When Grace gets his big shot, he bolts for the exit. That's the second surprise. Still, she chases him out.

In a few scenes, Project Hail Mary subtly introduces the three arcs of transformation that will guide the story: a pursuit of scientific research, professional collaborations that bloom into friendship, and a character who doesn't believe in himself. Without drawing notice, the film's clever structure intertwines all three lines together – and keeps surprising us.

  • GRACE
  • It took scientists two centuries to understand how bacteria work.

Humanity doesn't have two hundred years to learn about astrophage, the mysterious substance dimming the sun, so Grace is forced to compress centuries of research into a decade. Just our luck! A large part of Project Hail Mary's magic is how it uncovers new information about astrophage, putting fictional facts together in a way that feels like completing a puzzle. Even more impressive, each puzzle piece around astrophage pushes the plot forward or sets the characters back. Project Hail Mary slyly introduces the masses to the thrills and the setbacks of scientific discovery.

Along his way, Grace makes two friends, one in his Earth story and one out in space. On Earth, Sandra Hüller's character, the enigmatic Eva Stratt, keeps her cards close. She is a stoic figure, who speaks her mind at every opportunity, but rarely conveys what she is feeling. As the leader of a global initiative – the Project Hail Mary – her non-display is more professional duty than personal choice. She can't slow down.

  • GRACE
  • Do you believe in God?

  • STRATT
  • It's better than the alternative.

Even Stratt has surprises for us. For one scene only, she wears her emotions on her sleeve. During a team karaoke night, she sings to the group, astonishing us with her vocal range and then leaving us breathless with her song choice: Harry Styles's Sign of the Times. It's a song about being courageous as you face the end of the world.

None of this was in the script. The directors brainstormed the scene during the shoot and proposed the scene to Hüller, who asked to pick the song. They filmed without knowing where it would lead. When the song came on, all the actors and crew were reacting to it for the first time. Is this really happening? Their characters are seeing their stoic leader be vulnerable. She feels just like we do. At the peak of our rapture, she cuts the performance off.

  • STRATT
  • That's enough.

With as Irish a goodbye as she could make from center stage, Stratt leaves the party early. Her spontaneous arrival, brief performance, and early exit fit her character. It's a message to Grace: Hard things are supposed to be hard. Be strong.

Dialogue and scenes like this – all improvised during the shoot – are proof that Project Hail Mary is a perfect match of creative talent and material.

Grace's second friend makes the movie. Our first encounter with him is a jump scare, where he slams a window of solid xenon. In hindsight, we realize he slammed his mouth/butt on a window to point at something. That is the arc of transformation for Rocky, the personable rock creature from the planet Erud whose presence we grow to cherish. Though, when he eats, I will look the other way.

Rocky is unlike the aliens we traditionally see on screen. There are no wide eyes, slimy bodies, big heads, long antennas, or greenish gray skin tones. In fact, there are no eyes or skin to be found. To a human, Rocky is an animate rock. Much to our surprise, that poses no barrier to his blossoming friendship with Grace – or our affection.

Initially, the two are scared of each other. After a quick dance, they communicate through models. Grace starts to record Rocky's voice and create an audio dictionary. (We assume Grace knows how to program, which is lovely!) They start using words, which allows them to research astrophage together, and leads to shenanigans on Grace's ship.

  • GRACE
  • Personal space is at a premium.

Rocky's entrance changes the archetype of the story from man versus nature to friends versus nature. Project Hail Mary surprises us again by becoming a buddy movie wrapped in Andy Weir's style of science-fiction. To save their suns from astrophage, they have to save each other.

The third arc of transformation is Grace's internal journey from self-doubt to self-belief. Before his space voyage, Stratt tells him straight: If the sun dims, the food supply will shrink, the nations of the world will not ration food, and people will starve. In space, Grace is the last man standing, the only human between us and devastation. Yet, he flees from the challenge.

Space, however, is its own obstacle. There is no where to go. He is forced to don a suit and go on a space-walk. He is forced to enter an alien tunnel and meet Rocky. He is forced to continue his research on astrophage. Slowly, the denial fades and the weight of the mission grows on him.

That is why the shot of Grace cloaked in shadow, stepping into the light is an image that will last. In his tears, we finally see Grace accepting the reality of his mission – and the fact that Rocky is now his best friend in the universe.

  • ROCKY
  • I thought you make peace.

  • GRACE
  • I didn’t mean any of that. That’s just stuff you say.

Grace hugs Rocky's sphere. Sometimes, to be courageous, it takes someone to be courageous for.

Part of the viewing experience of Project Hail Mary is Daniel Pemberton's musical score. Over 75% of the film is layered with his work. It is experimental, merging an ethereal organ, wood blocks, a children's choir, electric guitar, drums, and a traditional orchestra. For example, listen to the track Wake Up Buddy. Even if we don't recognize any of the instruments, we all instantly recognize it as Rocky's theme.

I had the unique pleasure of viewing Project Hail Mary on opening weekend in a sold-out IMAX theater. It felt like any sporting event, concert, or stage performance – and maybe more pure. The crowd was laughing, going silent, and shedding tears as one. In that auditorium, there was no daylight between Ryland Grace’s journey and us.

When the film closed, there was a feeling of satisfaction that only comes from a well-told story. Yet, there was something else mixed in there, too. Project Hail Mary has so much going on, that it is able to keep the focus on the plot, without drawing our attention to what the story means. That excavation comes after.

With Grace and Rocky's scientific work, they are able to solve the crisis of astrophage and save their suns from dimming. Their research involved two species who hail from planets lightyears apart. Their journey gives us hope that, if we work together, big things are possible. If Grace and Rocky can collaborate and sacrifice themselves, there is no reason why humans on the same planet can't get big things done.

For a cast of characters who are called to save the world, what we get is surprising. A German bureaucrat working in secret whose name the general public will never know. An alien rock creature whose crew vanished, leaving him as the final survivor. A gifted researcher who gave up on his dreams because he lost his belief in himself. Think of the difference from where they begin and where they end up. More often than we suspect, courage is found in the most unexpected places.

Project Hail Mary takes the current moment head-on, with its rancor, unease, and non-cooperation, and shows us a way out. For our weary spirits, the film is a tonic that reminds us: If we work together, we can solve our problems.
