Good Luck, Have Fun, Don't Die

February 22, 2026 - 9:41 PM - Standard

They are coming.

Who's ready to SAVE THE FUTURE?!

The AI onslaught is here.

When the internet began, almost everything on it was made by humans and intended for other humans. In 2026, so much attention is being stolen by AI bots. This is content made by AI bots intended for humans. Now, the internet is no longer our own. When the AI bots crowd out the humans and begin to monopolize our attention, what happens next?

The story of Good Luck, Have Fun, Don’t Die transforms this question into drama, conjuring futuristic situations that don’t seem so far-fetched. These dramatic moments have all the elements of satire: part humor, part shock value, part tragedy, and wholly truthful.

Rather than spoil the surprises in the film, here is an invented situation with a similar tone: A white-collar job candidate logs on to a virtual interview. The hiring manager joins. While speaking, the hiring manager stutters mid-sentence. Her shirt color changes from green to purple. Then, her face mutates in look. The candidate detects that the hiring manager isn’t human, but an AI bot — and promptly leaves.

Rather than report a streak of candidates leaving early, the AI bot reports to the real hiring manager that none were good fits. The situation continues for months. As the job goes unfilled by a human, the real hiring manager automates the job and gives it to a bot.

Yes, that is the world we are barreling towards. It’s funny, it’s tragic, and it’s honest to the current moment. That’s what Good Luck, Have Fun, Don’t Die feels like to watch, presenting possible futures in a tangible way.

The script capitalizes on the most important rule in sci-fi: Ground situations in character. To extend the job interview example, we spend time with the job candidate — and their struggle to get hired. Then, we spend time with the real hiring manager — and their inability to find someone to hire. The AI bot is the one in the middle, stirring the pot to its advantage.

Good Luck, Have Fun, Don’t Die disarms us by introducing characters with humor. We learn about their world. When we see how characters are impacted by new technology, we feel the emotion. We begin to see the consequences that new technology can have on our world. This immediacy — the “Oh, shit!” moments in this movie — elevates the film into a cinematic op-ed.

The humor subsides, but the questions linger. The film asks big questions: What is the meaning of life, if our reality isn’t real? In a VR simulation of life, nothing is permanent. Death is undoable, so there are no consequences. At that point, what is the value of existence?

If we sit around all day, consuming content on social media made by bots, then what continues to make us human — and different from bots?

Sometimes, in eras of rapid change, the best course of action is slowing down. Think. Plan. Deliberate.

In the AI era, Good Luck, Have Fun, Don’t Die forces us to confront challenging questions. It manages to be artistic and provide a public service — by making the AI discussion accessible to all. Anyone can ask the big questions after they watch this film.

The movie’s title is verbose for a good reason. We are rewarded for remembering it. When that moment comes, the perspective of the film has come into full view: the filmmakers are pessimistic about humanity’s present course. All that we see today — or choose to tune out from — is a harbinger for much worse to come.

I went into this film on a total whim. A comedy about AI? The actor Sam Rockwell? Director Gore Verbinski of Pirates of the Caribbean? That’s all we need. The less you know about this film, the better.

Rest assured: This is a solidly constructed sci-fi film with a big heart and notable influences from the best in the genre.

My advice when you see it? Good Luck, Have Fun, Don’t Die.
