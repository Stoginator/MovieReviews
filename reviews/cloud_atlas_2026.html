Cloud Atlas

January 25, 2026 - 12:43 PM - Standard

An experimental, mainstream movie.

I can’t explain it, but I knew when I walked in that door…

2012’s Cloud Atlas is a unique and ambitious film. It has earned a reputation as “a hard nut to crack.” I can see why.

For starters, the film’s style is unapologetically bold. Featuring six storylines from different eras, the same cast of familiar actors play different characters in each story. Their dialogue and voiceover can be philosophical and hard to grasp, especially on a first watch. Building six worlds — each with their own characters, conflicts, and locations — in three hours means there is little lull time.

Because of that, my viewing experience was its own journey. The film’s playbook is revealed in the film’s first five minutes. I counted: 15 characters, 6 locations, 300 seconds. When the title card arrived, I paused the film. What just happened? I took that as my cue to rewind to the beginning.

After watching the opening sequence again, I still felt an inability to grasp the material. I’ll just deal with it, I thought. I continued with the film, encouraged by certain beats, indifferent to others. An hour in, sleep beckoned me — and I was in no mood to resist.

The following morning, I was well-rested. I returned to this ambitious film, curious to see where these six storylines go. And, something clicked. It felt like my brain processed the narrative overnight and prepared me for the rest. I rewound the film. Rather than fighting the construction of Cloud Atlas, I accepted it.

At each stage of its existence, Cloud Atlas was rejected. The production financing was always in question. In the public arena, the box-office performance was poor and the ambivalent critical reception did not help. Given all the obstacles, I am amazed that this film was ever made and released by a major Hollywood studio.

Consider the risk: A set of intertwining stories set across five hundred years, famous actors in unrecognizable makeup, men dressed as women, women dressed as men, and philosophical dialogue — all spanning multiple genres. In Cloud Atlas, there is a historical drama, a half-finished love story, an investigative thriller, a domestic drama, and two sci-fi epics.

Yet, it exists! It was made on a budget that seems to have protected the core vision. Tom Hanks and the triple team of writer-directors Lana Wachowski, Lilly Wachowski, and Tom Tykwer willed it into existence.

The stories in Cloud Atlas tackle our biggest questions: Are certain souls destined to be together? Do our actions in one life influence our luck in the next?

By crosscutting six stories together, Cloud Atlas packs these questions into an action-adventure experience — using the genre to provide the space it needs to suggesting these themes without being heavy-handed.

The crosscuts are quick! A scene in one story can last less than a minute before re-entering another story. While it is incredibly jarring on a first viewing, stick with it. There is an incredibly cinematic effect that builds.

Normally, a viewer associates a different level of tension to separate dramatic situations. For example, a story about characters embarking on a political revolution carries a different weight than a story about a parent trying to escape hospice. Cloud Atlas blurs our mental association, making completely separate circumstances feel connected and remarkably similar in tension.

Structurally, crosscuts work best when characters exist at the same time, navigating different places. Think back on any car chase — as the camera cuts between both drivers. Each driver’s conflict is one side of the same coin: either to catch or to not be caught.

However, if the characters exist at different times, then their conflict must be thematically connected. In Cloud Atlas, characters are in different worlds, facing unrelated obstacles in a literal sense. Thematically, their obstacles are all related: A search for freedom.

In each story, each actor embodies the same soul in a different time. Their familiar (or unrecognizable) face is the audience’s tell. No soul is pure or always just, but a mixture of experiences.

This is a Western film that deals in Eastern concepts like reincarnation and dharma, the idea of a soul’s purpose that stretches across lifetimes. The film finds heroes in everyday people, who do not come from nobility, wealth, or even a human mother. While I didn’t find the catharsis I expected at the end, I have much to ponder.

I am haunted by one unexplored encounter. At a party, a man and a woman briefly make eye contact. The man is a menacing mobster, with an unwelcoming disposition. There’s something about this woman that draws his gaze. The woman is more open, sensing something about that man, something deep within. There is a magnetic pull.

The man is Tom Hanks and the woman is Halle Berry.

In past and future stories, these characters meet again — discovering things they would not have found alone. In this story, however, they only trade glances. What could have been?

This cinematic moment lasts a few seconds in a nearly three-hour film. Yet, it made me wonder about my own story: What paths did I not travel? What led me here? Where will I go?

Cloud Atlas is an experience solely about such questions. On my next viewing, I wonder what I will find in this world.
